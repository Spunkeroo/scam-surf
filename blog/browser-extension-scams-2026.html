The Browser Extension Threat Landscape

Browser extensions have become one of the most underestimated vectors for cyberattacks. While most users carefully consider what apps they install on their phones, they add browser extensions with far less scrutiny despite the fact that extensions often have access to everything you do in your browser -- every website you visit, every password you enter, every form you fill out, and every financial transaction you conduct online.

The scale of the problem is significant. Security researchers estimate that millions of users have installed at least one malicious or deceptive browser extension. Google removed over 400 malicious Chrome extensions in a single enforcement action in 2024, and similar removals happen regularly. The Chrome Web Store alone hosts over 200,000 extensions, and while Google employs both automated and manual review processes, malicious extensions consistently bypass these defenses using sophisticated evasion techniques.

The consequences of installing a malicious extension range from annoying (ad injection, search hijacking) to devastating (stolen banking credentials, cryptocurrency theft, complete account takeover). Unlike traditional malware that must exploit vulnerabilities to gain access, extensions receive their access through permissions that users grant during installation -- permissions that most users do not read or understand.

Extension Security Alert

A single malicious browser extension can access every password you type, every credit card number you enter, and every website you visit. It operates within your browser with the permissions you granted during installation, often bypassing antivirus software because it technically has "authorized" access. Treat extension installation with the same caution you would apply to installing software on your computer.

Types of Malicious Extensions

Credential Stealers

These extensions monitor web pages for login forms and capture usernames, passwords, and authentication tokens as they are entered. Because the extension operates within the browser, it can read form data before encryption occurs, making HTTPS irrelevant as a defense. Some credential-stealing extensions are sophisticated enough to target specific high-value sites like banking portals, cryptocurrency exchanges, and email providers while appearing to function normally on other sites.

Ad Injectors and Search Hijackers

Ad injection extensions modify web pages to insert additional advertisements or replace existing ads with ones that generate revenue for the extension developer. Search hijackers redirect your searches through intermediary services that insert affiliate links or paid results before displaying real search results. While less immediately dangerous than credential stealers, these extensions degrade your browsing experience, slow page loading, and can expose you to malicious advertisements.

Cryptocurrency Miners and Wallet Drainers

Some extensions secretly use your computer's processing power to mine cryptocurrency for the developer, causing performance degradation and increased electricity costs. More dangerous are wallet-draining extensions that monitor clipboard contents and replace cryptocurrency addresses when you copy-paste them, redirecting transactions to the attacker's wallet. Some extensions specifically target users of popular crypto platforms, modifying transaction details in real-time.

Data Harvesters

Data harvesting extensions collect your browsing history, search queries, form data, and online behavior, which is then sold to data brokers or used for targeted phishing campaigns. Some extensions that appear to offer privacy features (like VPN or ad-blocking) actually collect and sell the very data they claim to protect.

How Malicious Extensions Operate

Delayed activation: Many malicious extensions behave completely normally for days or weeks after installation, passing both automated reviews and initial user scrutiny. Malicious functionality is activated only after a delay period or after receiving a command from a remote server. This makes it difficult for store review processes to detect the malicious behavior during evaluation.

Code obfuscation: Malicious code within extensions is heavily obfuscated to resist analysis. Variable names are randomized, functionality is split across multiple files and functions, and critical code may be downloaded dynamically from remote servers rather than included in the extension package that is reviewed by the store.

Legitimate acquisition: In a growing trend, attackers purchase established, trusted extensions from their original developers. An extension with 100,000 users and positive reviews is acquired, and a subsequent update introduces malicious code. Users who trusted the original extension are now running malware. This tactic has been documented in multiple high-profile cases.

Permission escalation: Some extensions launch with minimal permissions and legitimate functionality, build a user base and positive reviews, then request additional permissions through an update. Users who have come to rely on the extension may grant expanded permissions without careful consideration.

Major Extension Scams in 2025-2026

Critical

AI Assistant Extension Scams

The explosion of interest in AI tools has created a wave of fraudulent browser extensions claiming to provide ChatGPT, Claude, or other AI assistant functionality. Many of these extensions request extensive permissions, collect browsing data, and inject tracking code into every page you visit. Some have been found to contain credential-stealing code targeting specific banking and crypto platforms. Google removed dozens of fake AI assistant extensions in late 2025 after they accumulated millions of downloads.

High Risk

VPN and Privacy Extension Fraud

Fake VPN extensions promise to protect your privacy while actually collecting and selling your browsing data. Some route your traffic through servers controlled by the extension developer, enabling man-in-the-middle attacks. Ironically, users seeking privacy protection end up with less privacy than they would have had without the extension. Several popular free VPN extensions have been found collecting and monetizing user data despite promising anonymity.

Understanding Extension Permissions

The permissions an extension requests are the single most important factor in evaluating its safety. Understanding what each permission means helps you identify extensions that request more access than they need:

  • "Read and change all your data on all websites" -- This is the most powerful and dangerous permission. It allows the extension to see everything you do in your browser and modify any page. Only grant this to extensions you deeply trust and that genuinely need it.
  • "Read your browsing history" -- Allows the extension to see every site you have visited. Few extensions legitimately need this.
  • "Manage your downloads" -- Can initiate downloads without your explicit permission. Could be used to download malware.
  • "Read and change your data on specific sites" -- Less dangerous than all-site access but still allows the extension to manipulate your experience on the listed sites.
  • "Manage your apps, extensions, and themes" -- Allows the extension to install, disable, or modify other extensions. This is extremely dangerous and should almost never be needed.

How to Evaluate Extension Safety

Extension Safety Checklist
  • Check the developer: Research the developer's name and website. Established developers with a portfolio of extensions are more trustworthy than anonymous developers with a single extension.
  • Read recent reviews: Focus on negative reviews, particularly those mentioning unexpected behavior, privacy concerns, or changes after updates. Be aware that positive reviews can be fabricated.
  • Verify user count and age: Extensions with large user bases and long histories are generally safer, though acquired extensions can become malicious.
  • Evaluate permissions: Does the extension need all the permissions it requests? A dark mode extension should not need access to all your data on all websites.
  • Check update frequency: Regular updates indicate active development. But be cautious of sudden updates after long periods of inactivity, which may indicate the extension has been sold.
  • Look for open source: Extensions with publicly available source code on GitHub or similar platforms can be audited by the security community.
  • Use Extension Source Viewer: Chrome extensions like CRX Viewer let you inspect an extension's source code before installation.

Protecting Yourself

  • Minimize extensions: Every extension is a potential attack vector. Only install extensions you genuinely need and actively use. Remove any you no longer use.
  • Audit regularly: Review your installed extensions periodically. Check for any you do not recognize or no longer need. Verify that existing extensions have not changed ownership or behavior.
  • Use separate browser profiles: Use one browser profile for sensitive activities (banking, email) with minimal extensions, and another for general browsing with additional extensions.
  • Monitor permissions: When an extension requests new permissions through an update, carefully evaluate whether the new permissions are justified before granting them.
  • Prefer built-in features: Many browser features that previously required extensions (ad blocking, dark mode, screenshot tools) are now built into modern browsers. Built-in features are safer than third-party extensions.
  • Report suspicious extensions: If you encounter a malicious or deceptive extension, report it to the browser's extension store to protect other users.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do malicious browser extensions steal data?
Malicious extensions exploit the broad permissions they request during installation. They can read and modify all data on websites you visit, intercept form submissions including passwords and credit card numbers, redirect search queries through affiliate links, inject advertisements into web pages, track your complete browsing history, access cookies and session tokens, and modify or replace cryptocurrency wallet addresses during transactions.
How can I tell if a browser extension is safe?
Check the developer's reputation and history. Read recent reviews focusing on negative ones. Examine the permissions requested and ensure they match the extension's stated purpose. Verify the extension has a significant number of users and consistent update history. Check if the extension's source code is available for review. Be wary of extensions that request access to all websites when their function should only require access to specific sites.
What permissions should browser extensions NOT need?
A simple productivity extension should not need access to all your data on all websites, access to your browsing history, ability to modify data you copy and paste, access to your downloads, or ability to manage your other extensions. If a calculator extension asks to read your data on all websites, that is a major red flag. Always apply the principle of least privilege.
Can Chrome Web Store extensions contain malware?
Yes. Despite Google's review process, malicious extensions regularly appear on the Chrome Web Store. Google removed over 400 malicious extensions in a single enforcement action in 2024. Scammers use techniques like delayed payload activation, obfuscated code, and legitimate-looking front-end functionality to bypass reviews.
What should I do if I installed a malicious extension?
Remove the extension immediately. Change passwords for all accounts you accessed while the extension was installed, starting with banking and email. Enable two-factor authentication. Clear your browser cookies and cache. Run a full antivirus scan. Monitor financial accounts for unauthorized transactions. Check browser settings for changes to your default search engine, homepage, or proxy settings.
